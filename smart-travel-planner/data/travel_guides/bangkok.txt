Bangkok Travel Guide - The City of Angels

Overview
Bangkok, Thailand's capital, is a vibrant metropolis where ornate temples coexist with modern skyscrapers, and street food stalls sit beside luxury shopping malls. Known as Krung Thep (City of Angels) in Thai, Bangkok pulses with energy 24 hours a day. The city rewards adventurous travelers with incredible food, rich culture, and unforgettable experiences at remarkably affordable prices.

Best Time to Visit
November to February is the cool season with pleasant temperatures around 25-32 degrees Celsius and lower humidity. March to May is the hot season when temperatures can exceed 40 degrees. June to October is the rainy season with afternoon downpours, though it means fewer tourists and lower hotel prices. The rain usually comes in short, heavy bursts and rarely disrupts an entire day.

Must-See Attractions
The Grand Palace is Bangkok's most famous landmark, a dazzling complex of buildings used for royal ceremonies. The Emerald Buddha Temple (Wat Phra Kaew) within the palace grounds is Thailand's most sacred temple. Dress modestly with covered shoulders and long pants or skirts.

Wat Pho houses the magnificent 46-meter reclining Buddha covered in gold leaf. The temple is also home to the country's first public university and a renowned Thai massage school. Get a traditional Thai massage at the temple complex after exploring.

Wat Arun (Temple of Dawn) sits beautifully on the west bank of the Chao Phraya River. Its intricately decorated spire is covered in colorful porcelain. Cross the river by ferry for just a few baht. The temple is especially stunning at sunset.

Chatuchak Weekend Market is one of the world's largest outdoor markets with over 15,000 stalls. You can find everything from vintage clothing and handicrafts to plants and street food. Go early to beat the heat and crowds. Bargaining is expected.

The floating markets outside Bangkok, such as Damnoen Saduak and Amphawa, offer a glimpse into traditional Thai commerce. Amphawa is less touristy and more authentic, open on weekends.

Hidden Gems
Chinatown (Yaowarat) comes alive at night with some of the best street food in the city. Try the seafood at T&K Seafood, fresh oyster omelets, and mango sticky rice. The area's gold shops, herbal medicine stores, and temples add to the atmosphere.

The Jim Thompson House is a beautiful example of traditional Thai architecture, home to the American silk entrepreneur who mysteriously disappeared in 1967. The garden setting provides a peaceful escape from the city.

Bang Krachao is nicknamed Bangkok's Green Lung - a jungle-like peninsula accessible by a short boat ride. Rent a bicycle and explore quiet paths through gardens, temples, and a floating market. It feels a world away from the city.

Khao San Road is the legendary backpacker street, offering cheap accommodation, street food, market stalls, and nightlife. Even if you are not staying there, an evening visit is an entertaining cultural experience.

Food and Dining
Bangkok is widely considered one of the world's greatest food cities. Street food is king here, with incredible dishes available for as little as 40-80 baht.

Must-try dishes include pad thai (stir-fried noodles), som tum (green papaya salad), tom yum goong (spicy shrimp soup), green curry, mango sticky rice, boat noodles, and khao man gai (chicken rice). For the adventurous, try grilled insects from street vendors.

Jay Fai is a legendary street food stall that earned a Michelin star for its crab omelet and drunken noodles. Expect long waits but the food is worth it. Many other Michelin Bib Gourmand restaurants offer incredible value.

Rooftop bars are a Bangkok specialty. Sky Bar at Lebua State Tower (featured in The Hangover Part II), Octave Rooftop Bar, and Vertigo are among the most famous.

Transportation
The BTS Skytrain and MRT subway are the fastest ways to navigate the city. Buy a Rabbit card for the BTS for easy tap-and-go travel. Both systems connect at several interchange stations.

Tuk-tuks are iconic but negotiate the price before getting in. Motorcycle taxis (identified by orange vests) are fast but thrilling. The Chao Phraya Express Boat is an efficient and scenic way to travel along the river.

Taxis are metered and affordable. Always insist the driver uses the meter. Grab (Southeast Asia's ride-hailing app) is widely used and often more convenient than hailing a taxi.

Budget Tips
Bangkok is one of the most affordable major cities in the world. Street food meals cost under $2 USD. Budget hotels and hostels are available from $10-20 per night. Many temples have small entry fees of just 20-100 baht.

Visit the free temples and markets. Take public boats and the BTS/MRT instead of taxis. Eat where locals eat for the best quality at the lowest prices. Avoid tourist restaurants in heavily trafficked areas.

Buy Thai massage sessions at local shops rather than hotel spas for a fraction of the price. Many excellent massage shops charge just 200-300 baht for an hour.

Cultural Tips
Show respect for the Thai royal family - it is illegal to criticize the monarchy. Remove shoes before entering temples and homes. Do not touch anyone's head, as it is considered the most sacred part of the body. Do not point your feet at people or Buddha images.

The Thai greeting is the wai - placing palms together at chest level with a slight bow. Return a wai when greeted this way. Learn to say "sawadee krap/ka" (hello) and "khop khun krap/ka" (thank you).
